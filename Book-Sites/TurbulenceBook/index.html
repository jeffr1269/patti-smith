From the Book

What is transactional turbulence?

If you are buying or selling a home, you can probably already feel it, the quiet anxiety just under the excitement. Patti calls it transactional turbulence: the unseen currents in contracts, contingencies, appraisals, inspections, timelines, lenders, and emotions that can delay, derail, or completely dissolve a deal.

She has identified 116 distinct kinds, most of them invisible until they hit. This book is a behind-the-scenes look at each one, from both the buyer's and the seller's side, and a reminder that who guides you through the turbulence matters more than ever.

Think of it as a compass. Read the turbulence that speaks to your situation, and know you do not have to navigate any of it alone. The full index of all 116 is below.

Fudging the truth on a loan application might seem like a harmless way to secure your dream home, but it’s a slippery slope that can lead to serious consequences. Whether it’s exaggerating income, hiding debt, or fabricating financial support, misrepresenting information puts the entire transaction at risk.

From the borrower’s perspective, the pressure to qualify can be overwhelming. You might think, “Who’s going to check?” The answer: the lender. Mortgage underwriters are trained to verify every detail, and when they uncover discrepancies, your loan could be denied outright. Even if the lie isn’t discovered until after closing, the lender could call the loan due, leaving you facing foreclosure, or worse, legal trouble.

From the seller’s perspective, it’s frustrating. They’ve taken their home off the market and made plans, and now the deal is falling apart because the buyer wasn’t honest. Their timeline is derailed, and trust is broken.

What is the Lesson? Telling the truth might feel harder at the moment, but it keeps the process smooth and protects your financial future. Lenders are there to help you find a home you can afford, not one built on shaky foundations of misrepresentation.

Recent late payments on your credit report might not seem like a big deal, but in the world of mortgage lending, they’re a flashing warning sign. Even a single late payment can send ripples through the home-buying process, affecting your approval, interest rate, and the trust of everyone involved.

From the borrower’s perspective, late payments often happen due to unexpected financial hiccups or simple forgetfulness. However, when the lender reviews your credit history, those missed payments can lower your credit score and raise concerns about your ability to handle a mortgage. This might lead to a higher interest rate or, in some cases, a denial of your loan.

From the seller’s perspective, it’s a frustrating revelation. They’ve likely accepted your offer in good faith, assuming your financing was solid. When late payments complicate or stall the transaction, their plans, and potentially their finances, are put on hold.

What is the Lesson? Timely bill payments are critical, especially in the months leading up to a home purchase. If you have recent late payments, be transparent with your lender upfront and work on strategies to improve your creditworthiness. Buying a home is about trust, and staying current on payments builds confidence with lenders, sellers, and everyone involved.

Discovering additional debt after a loan application is like adding extra luggage to an already packed car, it throws everything off balance. For lenders, this new information can undermine the entire loan approval process, leaving everyone scrambling to pick up the pieces.

From the borrower’s perspective, taking on new debt might have seemed harmless, maybe it was a new car loan, a maxed-out credit card, or a personal loan to cover moving costs. But lenders reassess your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio before finalizing a loan, and that unexpected debt could tip the scales. You might lose your loan approval or face a higher interest rate.

From the seller’s perspective, it’s a frustrating and potentially deal-breaking scenario. They’ve likely made plans based on your ability to close, and now their timeline is in jeopardy. Delays or the prospect of finding a new buyer can add unnecessary stress to their experience.

What is the Lesson? Don’t take on new debt after applying for a mortgage, none, not even a small store credit card. Be transparent with your lender about all existing obligations upfront. Financial stability is critical during the loan process, and avoiding additional debt ensures a smoother path to homeownership.

From the seller’s perspective, this is an unexpected and frustrating twist. They’ve taken their home off the market, planned their next steps, and trusted the buyer’s ability to close. Now they’re left waiting, or worse, searching for a new buyer, while time and money slip away.

What is the Lesson? Life happens, but swift action, honesty, and collaboration can sometimes keep the deal afloat, even in turbulent times.
